# 🧑‍🏫 Mentor Guide — The 15-Minutes-a-Day Playbook

**You do not need to know how to code.** Your job is encouragement, accountability, and being there for two short check-ins each day. This guide tells you exactly what to do.

---

## Your two daily check-ins

### ⭐ Kickoff (first ~10 minutes of the day)
1. Sit with the student and open the day's `README.md` together.
2. Read the **🎯 Goal** line out loud.
3. Do the **🧩 Warm-Up** puzzle together (it's quick and screen-free or playful).
4. Say: *"I'll be back for Show & Tell. Use the hints if you get stuck, and remember — looking at the answer to understand it is allowed."*
5. Leave them to work. **Resist the urge to hover.** Productive struggle is where the learning happens.

### ⭐ Show & Tell (last ~15 minutes of the day)
Have the student **run their program and explain it to you.** Use these questions:

- "Show me it running. What does it do?"
- "What's one thing you **changed or added** today?"
- "What was the **trickiest part**, and how did you get past it?"
- "If you had 10 more minutes, what would you add?"

Then celebrate — high five, sticker, check the box in the main `README.md` tracker. **Praise effort and problem-solving, not just 'it worked.'**

---

## What to say when they're stuck (without knowing code)

You can help a lot without reading Python. Try these, in order:

1. **"Read me the error out loud — what line number does it mention?"** (Then have them look at that line.)
2. **"What did you expect to happen, and what happened instead?"** (Naming it often reveals the fix.)
3. **"Have you checked the Hints at the bottom of the README?"** (They reveal help gradually.)
4. **"Try the rubber duck trick: explain your code to me line by line like I'm a 5-year-old."** (They'll usually spot the bug themselves mid-sentence.)
5. **"You've tried for 15 minutes and used the hints — go ahead and read the `solution/` file, understand it, then retype it yourself."**

If they're still stuck after all that, it's a great real-world lesson to **write the question down** and look it up together later, or ask a friend/relative who codes.

---

## The most common beginner errors (so you're not surprised)

| What you'll see | What it usually means | Calm fix |
|---|---|---|
| `SyntaxError` | A typo — often a missing quote `"`, parenthesis `)`, or colon `:` | "Look at the line number — is a quote or bracket missing its partner?" |
| `IndentationError` | The spaces at the start of a line are off | "Python cares about spacing. Does this line line up with the others in its group?" |
| `NameError` | A misspelled variable name, or quotes missing around text | "Is that word spelled the same everywhere? Should it have quotes?" |
| `ValueError` from `int()` | They typed letters where a number was expected | "The program wanted a number — try typing a number." |
| Program "freezes" | An **infinite loop** | "Click the Terminal and press **Ctrl + C** to stop it." |

> 🔑 The single most valuable thing you can model: **errors are normal and not scary.** Stay relaxed and curious. Your calm sets their confidence.

## Week-by-week heads-up (where to lean in)

- **Before Week 1:** Do `SETUP.md` together — this is the #1 place you're truly needed (45–60 min).
- **Day 6 (indentation) & Day 8 (reading errors):** Consider sitting in for the Concept Quest, not just Kickoff. These two skills are best learned with someone.
- **Week 5 (functions):** A conceptual leap. Extra encouragement helps; the [CodingBat](https://codingbat.com/python) practice problems are great if they want more reps.
- **Day 21 (capstone planning):** Help them **pick a project that's small enough to finish.** Kids over-scope. "What's the simplest version that still works?" is your best question all camp.
- **Day 24 (showcase):** Make it a real event — invite family, let them present, print the certificate (`certificate-of-completion.md`).

---

## When a day goes sideways

- **Finishing every `# TODO` is never the goal.** Reaching the Build Block and understanding the idea is a win, even if the project isn't done.
- **A frustrated kid learns nothing.** If they're melting down, it's fine to stop early, do a typing game, or move the hard part to tomorrow.
- **Some days are short.** A 75-minute day where it clicked beats a miserable 2-hour grind.

You've got this. Mostly: show up, stay positive, and be genuinely impressed — because what they're doing is genuinely impressive. 🌟
